The frameworks that matter

12 PM Frameworks You'll Actually Use

These are the ones that come up in interviews and, honestly, the ones you'll use on the job. I've included where they came from and how to actually apply them.

Industry Standard

1. RICE Framework

Developed by Sean McBride at Intercom

The Formula

RICE Score = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort

Component Definitions

Reach: Number of people/events affected in a specific timeframe (e.g., "customers per quarter")
Impact: 3=Massive, 2=High, 1=Medium, 0.5=Low, 0.25=Minimal
Confidence: 100%=high with data, 80%=medium, 50%=gut feeling
Effort: Person-months required (1 week PM + 2 weeks eng = 0.75 months)

Real-World Example

Feature R I C E Score
Feature A 150 2 80% 1 240
Feature B 500 0.5 100% 3 83.3

Feature A wins despite lower reach due to higher impact and lower effort.

Pros
  • • Data-driven, reduces bias
  • • Provides ranked list
  • • Measures "total impact per time worked"
Cons
  • • Requires data that may not exist
  • • Reach/impact difficult to measure accurately
  • • Can be time-consuming
Agile Essential

2. MoSCoW Method

Created in 1994 by Dai Clegg at Oracle

Must-Have

Essential for success. Product fails without them.

Example: Seatbelts in cars

Should-Have

Important but not critical. Workarounds exist.

Example: Pedals, brakes

Could-Have

Nice-to-haves. First to be deprioritized.

Example: Horn, fun color

Won't-Have

Explicitly excluded. Prevents scope creep.

Example: Bluetooth speaker

Resource Allocation Rule

Common allocation: 60% Must-Have, 20% Should-Have, 20% Could-Have

Key Tip: Keep Must-Haves minimal. Challenge every item with: "Can we imagine the product succeeding without this?"

Customer Satisfaction

3. Kano Model

Developed in 1984 by Dr. Noriaki Kano at Tokyo University

Core Insight

Customer satisfaction is non-linear: not all features have equal impact. Emotional responses drive loyalty.

Must-Be (Basic)

Expected by default. Absence causes dissatisfaction, presence creates neutrality.

Example: Clean hotel room

Performance

Linear relationship: more means more satisfaction.

Example: Battery life, storage

Attractive (Delighters)

Disproportionate satisfaction. Absence doesn't cause dissatisfaction.

Example: First iPhone touchscreen

The Kano Questionnaire

For each feature, ask TWO questions:

Functional: "How would you feel if this feature WERE present?"
Dysfunctional: "How would you feel if this feature WERE NOT present?"

Response Scale: I like it • I expect it • I am neutral • I can tolerate it • I dislike it

Temporal Dynamics

Features migrate over time. AI capabilities were Attractive in 2023, becoming Performance in 2025, may become Must-Be by 2026.

5. Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

Popularized by Clayton Christensen (Harvard), conceptualized by Tony Ulwick

Core Theory

"People don't buy products; they 'hire' them to make progress in specific circumstances."

Famous Milkshake Example

A fast food company tried improving milkshakes based on demographics, but sales did not improve.

JTBD research revealed: Morning commuters hired milkshakes to "make boring commute more interesting" and "stave off hunger until lunch."

The job was not "taste good"; it was "keep me occupied and full during long drive."

Solution: Thicker shakes, faster service, loyalty cards.

Functional

The practical task

"Create a hole in wall"

Emotional

How it makes them feel

"Feel accomplished"

Social

How others perceive them

"Appear competent"

Critical JTBD Interview Questions
  • • "What job were you trying to get done?" (not "why did you buy this?")
  • • "What were you using before?" (reveals competitive set)
  • • "What made you switch?" (uncovers hiring criteria)
  • • "When did you first think about solving this?" (timeline of decision)

6. Opportunity Solution Tree (OST)

Created in 2016, part of Continuous Discovery framework

Four-Level Structure

1.
Desired Outcome The business metric you want to improve (e.g., increase retention by 20%)
2.
Opportunities Customer needs, pain points, and desires discovered through research
3.
Solutions Potential ways to address each opportunity
4.
Experiments Tests to validate assumptions about solutions
Key Benefits
  • • Prevents jumping straight to solutions before understanding problems
  • • Creates shared understanding across product trio (PM, designer, engineer)
  • • Makes implicit assumptions explicit
  • • Living document that evolves with learning
Design Thinking

7. Double Diamond

Introduced by British Design Council in 2005

Diamond 1: Problem Space

Discover (Diverge)

Explore broadly: user interviews, market research, stakeholder conversations

Define (Converge)

Narrow focus: synthesize insights, create clear problem statement

Diamond 2: Solution Space

Develop (Diverge)

Generate many solutions: brainstorming, prototyping, iteration

Deliver (Converge)

Select best solution: testing, refinement, launch

When to Use
  • • Complex problems requiring deep understanding before solution
  • • Cross-functional team alignment
  • • New product development or major redesigns
Modern Essential

8. Continuous Discovery Habits

Developed by Teresa Torres, codified in her 2021 book

Definition

"At minimum, weekly touchpoints with customers by the team building the product, conducting small research activities in pursuit of a desired product outcome."

Core Elements
  • • Weekly Customer Interviews: Sustainable habit of learning
  • • Product Trio Involvement: PM, designer, and tech lead together
  • • Automated Recruiting: Build recruitment into your product
  • • Interview Snapshots: One-page synthesis of each interview
  • • Experience Maps: Living documents of customer journey
Anti-Patterns to Avoid
  • • Relying on one person to recruit and interview
  • • Only interviewing when you think you need it
  • • Sharing what you learned by sending pages of notes
  • • Stopping everything to synthesize a set of interviews
Foundation

9. Scrum/Agile

The most common delivery framework in tech

Product Owner

Maximizes product value, manages backlog, communicates Product Goal

Scrum Master

Facilitates process, removes impediments, coaches team

Developers

Build usable increments each sprint

Scrum Events

Sprint Planning

Define Sprint Goal and select backlog items (max 8 hours for 1-month sprint)

Daily Scrum

15-minute daily inspection of progress toward Sprint Goal

Sprint Review

Demonstrate increment to stakeholders, gather feedback

Sprint Retrospective

Identify improvements for next sprint

Backlog Refinement

Ongoing activity (not an official Scrum event) to clarify, estimate, and prioritize backlog items. Goal: Have 1.5-2 sprints worth of refined work ready.

Modern Roadmapping

10. Now-Next-Later Roadmapping

Developed by Janna Bastow (CEO of ProdPad)

NOW

Items currently being executed

  • • High confidence
  • • Validated
  • • Detailed
  • • Through discovery
NEXT

Top priorities for short term

  • • Being researched
  • • Being validated
  • • Preparing for dev
LATER

Long-term strategic direction

  • • High-level
  • • Less detailed
  • • Reflects vision
Benefits
  • • Embraces uncertainty with no false precision on dates
  • • Easy to update as priorities shift
  • • Great for stakeholder communication
Update Cadence

Review every 6 weeks. Enough time to run a discovery or development phase, learn, and adjust.

Goal Setting

11. OKRs (Objectives & Key Results)

Developed at Intel by Andy Grove, popularized by John Doerr at Google

Structure

Objective

What you want to achieve. Qualitative, inspirational, time-bound.

Key Results

How you'll measure progress. Quantitative, 3-5 per objective, SMART.

Product OKR Example

Objective: Provide an easy onboarding experience

  • KR1: 90% of users complete onboarding in under 5 minutes
  • KR2: Fewer than 10% require customer support during onboarding
  • KR3: 30-day activation rate increases from 60% to 80%
Common Mistakes
  • • Confusing Key Results with tasks (outputs vs. outcomes)
  • • Setting too many OKRs (focus on 2-3 objectives)
  • • Making OKRs too easy (should be 70% achievable)
  • • Using OKRs for performance evaluation (reduces ambition)

1 RICE Calculation Worked Example

From the e-commerce exercise above:

Feature Reach Impact (0-3) Confidence Effort (months) RICE Score
One-click checkout 10,000 3 (High) 0.90 2 13,500
Product recommendations 50,000 2 (Medium) 0.70 4 17,500
Social sharing 5,000 1 (Low) 0.50 1 2,500

Formula: RICE = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort

Example: One-click = (10,000 × 3 × 0.90) / 2 = 13,500

Winner: Product Recommendations (17,500) despite longer effort. The high reach and medium impact outweigh the development time. However, if you need a quick win, One-click checkout (13,500) delivers high value in half the time.

          Worked Example: "Design a Fire Alarm for the Deaf"

          1. Clarification

          Residential or commercial? Let's assume residential.

          2. User Segmentation
          • • Hard of hearing (can hear loud noises)
          • • Profoundly deaf (cannot hear)
          • • Elderly deaf (may have mobility issues)
          3. Pain Points

          Traditional alarms rely on audio. Deaf person sleeping, in shower, or focusing visually might miss visual strobe.

          4. Solutions
          • A. Strobe Light Network (every room, synced)
          • B. Tactile Bed Shaker (vibrating pillow pad)
          • C. Olfactory Alarm (wasabi scent release)
          • D. Wearable Integration (smartwatch vibration)
          5. Prioritization

          Solution D (Wearable) is high adoption but battery-dependent. Solution B (Bed shaker) is fail-safe for sleeping, addressing the most dangerous scenario.

          → Prioritize: Bed Shaker + Strobe combo

          RICE Scoring

          Intercom's prioritization framework

          RICE = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort

          Impact Scale: 3=Massive, 2=High, 1=Medium, 0.5=Low, 0.25=Minimal

          Confidence: 100%=High, 80%=Medium, 50%=Low

          Project Reach Impact Conf Effort Score

          Project B500180%4100
          Project C12,000250%112,000

          Project A wins despite lower reach than C due to higher confidence

          MoSCoW Method

          MVP definition framework

          Must Core value; cannot launch without. Launch blocked if missing.
          Should Important but can work around. Launch acceptable without.
          Could Nice-to-have; add if time permits. Low priority.
          Won't Out of scope for this release. Explicitly excluded.

          Pro tip: Start with all features as "Won't Have" and work backwards

          Kano Model

          Customer satisfaction analysis

          Delighters: Unexpected features that create excitement (e.g., first iPhone touch screen)
          Performance: More is better: battery life, speed, storage
          Must-haves: Expected basics; absence causes dissatisfaction

          Feature Migration: Smartphone cameras: Delighter (2007) → Performance (2012) → Must-have (2020)

          Jobs to Be Done: The "Switch" Interview

          Analyzing why a user switched from Zoom to Google Meet:

          Push (Pain of Current)

          "Zoom's 40-minute limit on free plan cuts off my meetings."

          Pull (Appeal of New)

          "Google Meet is integrated directly into my Calendar invites."

          Anxiety (Fear of New)

          "Will video quality be as good? Will clients know how to use it?"

          Habit (Inertia)

          "I'm used to the Zoom interface and button placement."

          Application: To win this user, Google Meet must maximize the Pull (integration) while minimizing Anxiety ("One-click join, no download required").

          Quick Reference

          PM Glossary

          All the jargon, acronyms, and concepts you'll hear in interviews and on the job. Bookmark this one.

          RICE

          Framework

          Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort. Intercom's prioritization framework. Gives you a score to compare features objectively.

          MoSCoW

          Framework

          Must have, Should have, Could have, Won't have. Used for scoping MVPs and releases.

          AARRR (Pirate Metrics)

          Framework

          Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Revenue, Referral. The growth funnel for tracking user journey.

          OKRs

          Framework

          Objectives and Key Results. Goal-setting framework from Intel/Google. Objective = what you want to achieve. Key Results = how you'll measure if you got there.

          JTBD (Jobs to Be Done)

          Framework

          Users "hire" products to get a job done. Focus on the underlying need, not the feature request.

          Kano Model

          Framework

          Categorizes features by user satisfaction: Must-haves (expected), Performance (more is better), Delighters (unexpected wow). Features migrate over time.

          CIRCLES

          Framework

          Comprehend, Identify, Report, Cut, List, Evaluate, Summarize. Interview framework for product design questions.

          STAR Method

          Framework

          Situation, Task, Action, Result. Structure for behavioral interview answers.

          SWOT Analysis

          Framework

          Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats. Classic strategy framework. Internal (S/W) vs External (O/T). Good for competitive analysis.

          Porter's Five Forces

          Framework

          Analyzes industry competition: supplier power, buyer power, competitive rivalry, threat of substitutes, threat of new entrants. Useful for strategy questions.

          3Cs

          Framework

          Company, Customers, Competitors. Simple strategic analysis framework. What can we do? What do users need? What are others doing?

          OST (Opportunity Solution Tree)

          Framework

          Teresa Torres's framework. Maps: Desired Outcome → Opportunities → Solutions → Experiments. Keeps you focused on outcomes over outputs.

          DHM Model

          Framework

          Delight, Hard-to-copy, Margin-enhancing. Netflix's prioritization lens. Does it delight users? Is it defensible? Does it make money?

          North Star Metric

          Metric

          The single metric that best captures the value you deliver. Airbnb = Nights Booked. Spotify = Time Listening. Guides all decisions.

          DAU / MAU

          Metric

          Daily / Monthly Active Users. DAU/MAU ratio measures "stickiness," or how often people come back. Greater than 50% is strong for consumer apps.

          LTV (CLV)

          Metric

          Lifetime Value (Customer Lifetime Value). Total revenue from a customer over their entire relationship. Should be 3x+ your CAC.

          CAC

          Metric

          Customer Acquisition Cost. How much you spend to get one new customer. Marketing spend ÷ new customers. LTV:CAC of 3:1 is healthy.

          Churn Rate

          Metric

          Percentage of customers who stop using your product in a given period. The silent killer of SaaS businesses. Lower = better.

          Retention Rate

          Metric

          Percentage of users who come back after their first use. Day 1, Day 7, Day 30 retention. The inverse of churn, basically.

          Activation Rate

          Metric

          Percentage of new users who reach the "aha moment," the point where they get value. Sign-up does not count; first meaningful action does.

          NPS

          Metric

          Net Promoter Score. "How likely to recommend?" on 0-10 scale. Promoters (9-10) minus Detractors (0-6). Above 50 is excellent.

          KPI

          Metric

          Key Performance Indicator. Any metric you track to measure success. The important ones, not vanity metrics.

          Counter-Metric

          Metric

          A metric you watch to make sure you're not gaming your main metric. If you optimize for sign-ups, track activation too so you're not just getting junk users.

          Conversion Rate

          Metric

          Percentage of users who complete a desired action. Visitors → Sign-ups, Free → Paid, Add to Cart → Purchase. The funnel math.

          APM

          Role

          Associate Product Manager. Entry-level PM role. Often part of structured programs (Google, Meta, etc.). The way in for new grads and career switchers.

          IC

          Role

          Individual Contributor. Someone who does the work vs. manages others. You can be a very senior IC (Staff PM, Principal PM) without managing anyone.

          GPM / Director / VP

          Role

          Group PM, Director of Product, VP Product. Management track. GPM manages PMs. Director manages GPMs. VP owns a product area or the whole thing.

          CPO

          Role

          Chief Product Officer. The top of the PM ladder. Owns product strategy, reports to CEO. Not every company has one; sometimes it is a VP.

          Technical PM

          Role

          PM who works on infrastructure, APIs, developer tools, or AI/ML products. Usually needs engineering background. Higher technical bar in interviews.

          Growth PM

          Role

          PM focused on user acquisition, activation, and retention. Very data-driven, lots of A/B testing. Lives in the AARRR funnel.

          Platform PM

          Role

          PM who builds for other teams/developers, not end users directly. Internal tools, APIs, shared services. Your "users" are other engineers.

          MVP

          Process

          Minimum Viable Product. The smallest thing you can build to test if your idea works. Not "version 1"; it is an experiment, not a product launch.

          PRD

          Process

          Product Requirements Document. The spec that tells engineering what to build and why. Format varies wildly by company. Some use Notion, some use 1-pagers.

          Roadmap

          Process

          A plan of what you are building and (roughly) when. Lives in Jira, Productboard, or a spreadsheet. Should be flexible; it is a plan, not a promise.

          Backlog

          Process

          The list of everything you could build, prioritized. Top of backlog = doing soon. Bottom = "maybe someday." PM owns prioritization.

          Sprint

          Process

          A fixed time period (usually 2 weeks) where the team commits to shipping specific work. Part of Agile/Scrum. Ends with a demo and retro.

          A/B Test

          Process

          Show version A to some users, version B to others, see which performs better. The scientific method for product decisions. Needs statistical significance.

          Feature Flag

          Process

          A toggle that lets you turn features on/off for specific users without deploying new code. Used for gradual rollouts and A/B tests. Also called "feature toggle."

          Discovery vs Delivery

          Process

          Discovery: Figuring out what to build (research, prototypes). Delivery: Actually building it. Good teams run both in parallel.

          Dual Track Agile

          Process

          Running discovery and delivery simultaneously. While engineers build Sprint N, PM/Design validate ideas for Sprint N+1. Prevents the "spec waterfall."

          User Research

          Process

          Talking to users to understand their problems. Interviews, surveys, usability tests. "Get out of the building." The thing most PMs don't do enough of.

          Stakeholder

          Process

          Anyone with a vested interest in your product. Engineering, design, sales, legal, execs, customers. PM job = balancing all their needs.

          B2B / B2C / B2B2C

          Business

          Business-to-Business, Business-to-Consumer, Business-to-Business-to-Consumer. Who your customer is. Slack = B2B. Spotify = B2C. Stripe = B2B2C (serves businesses who serve consumers).

          SaaS

          Business

          Software as a Service. Subscription software delivered over the internet. Salesforce, Notion, Figma. Recurring revenue model.

          FAANG / MAANG

          Business

          Facebook/Meta, Amazon, Apple, Netflix, Google. The big tech companies everyone wants to work at. High pay, tough interviews. Sometimes includes Microsoft (MAANG).

          Two-Sided Marketplace

          Business

          Platform connecting two user groups. Airbnb (hosts + guests), Uber (drivers + riders). The chicken-and-egg problem: need both sides to have value.

          Liquidity

          Business

          In marketplaces: how quickly buyers find sellers (and vice versa). High liquidity = stuff sells fast. Low liquidity = listings sit forever. Critical marketplace metric.

          Unit Economics

          Business

          Revenue and costs per "unit" (usually per customer). If it costs $50 to acquire a customer who pays you $30, you lose money. LTV > CAC or you're toast.

          Product-Led Growth (PLG)

          Business

          Growth driven by the product itself, not sales teams. Users try free, get hooked, upgrade. Slack, Notion, Figma. Requires great onboarding.

          Moat
